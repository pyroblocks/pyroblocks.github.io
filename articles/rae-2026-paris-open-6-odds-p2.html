RAE Paris Open 6 Preview: EU, SAM, APAC

Who will secure the final tickets to the Paris Major?

April 22, 2026

This article was written in affiliation with ShiftRLE. It may differ somewhat from the style and content of other articles.

The RLCS RAE (Region-Adjusted Elo) Rating system is a predictive model for professional Rocket League, using updating team ratings to generate probabilities for matches, teams, and tournaments. You can read more about it here.

RLCS 2026 continues its march towards the stage of the Paris La Défense Arena as Europe, South America, and Asia-Pacific will send a total of eight teams to join the eight already qualified for the Paris Major through regional results around the world last weekend.

Open 6 in NA, MENA, OCE, and SSA followed the script (unlike the chaotic events before them), with the favorites for the region’s collective eight spots all securing tickets to Paris (and for five of them, the RLCS 2026 World Championship). Each region’s top-rated team (NRG Esports, Twisted Minds, Wildcard, Five Fears) also won the final regional of the season.

Here’s a preview of what lies in store in the final online regionals of RLCS 2026.

Europe

In Split 1, RLCS 2026 looked to be on track for another bog-standard season: Karmine Corp on top, Team Vitality and Gentle Mates right behind, and both Ninjas in Pyjamas (NIP) and Geekay Esports on the fringe of LAN qualification.

Though Manchester City Esports (MCI) made the race for EU’s fifth spot — secured after Karmine won the Kick-Off Weekend in December — interesting by taking Geekay to a tiebreaker series, the experienced roster of Jack "ApparentlyJack" Benton, Joseph "Joyo" Young, and Enzo "Seikoo" Grondein ended up securing their spot in Boston.

Geekay would collect a solid Top 8 result on LAN before making a roster move that would define Split 2 in EU so far: swapping Seikoo, who had a strong performance in Boston, with MCI’s Christian Fabricius "TempoH" Mortensen. Seikoo would join Aston "accro" Smith and William "ejby" Ejby Lorentzen for Split 2 on MCI.

Since the move, the two teams have swapped fortunes, and then some — MCI started off their split with a 3-0 sweep over defending major champions Gentle Mates on their road to back-to-back Top 4 placements, while Geekay has gone out on Friday twice at the hands of Yanis "Alpha54" Champenois’ Kaydop Corp.

After Team Vitality briefly flashed the best-in-the-world form that they showed at times in Boston in Open 4 (sweeping Inner Sircle, MCI, and NIP in the playoff bracket to claim their first regional win since RLCS 2024), Gentle Mates reclaimed their spot at the top of the EU pile in Open 5 with a grand finals victory over Karmine Corp (Team Vitality went out on Saturday to Kaydop Corp). It was a much needed victory after the Mates’ early exit in Open 4 thanks to dropping tough series in the GSL stage against both MCI and Karmine.

Though they’ve been fighting for the top spot in EU with both Gentle Mates and Vitality, Karmine Corp have been more of a beacon of consistency than the top dog in Split 2, with a Top 4 result (at the hands of a resurgent NIP) and a second place to show for their efforts in Opens 4 and 5.

NIP answered questions about their staying power at the top of EU with a resounding win over Karmine in the Open 4 semifinals before nearly repeating the trick at the same stage in Open 5 after taking down Vitality in the upper semifinals of the GSL stage.

After chaos in the GSL stage scrambled the playoff bracket in Open 4, the top teams in Europe largely returned to relevance in Open 5; the only one other than the region’s five best to make playoffs in both events has been Kaydop Corp, who fell to MCI in lower round one of Open 4 and Karmine in the quarterfinals Open 5 (after defeating Vitality in a Game 7 series).

Thanks to MCI (16 points), NIP (20), and Karmine (20) repeating Top 4 or better results between Open 4 and Open 5, and both Gentle Mates (20) and Vitality (22) claiming regional titles, the race for the Paris Major in Europe leaves only the narrowest of paths for any teams trying to break into the conversation. Only Kaydop Corp (10) controls their destiny outside of the five teams currently holding spots.

RAE Rankings — EU Open 6

RAE, narrowly, rates Vitality as the best team in Europe, followed very closely by Gentle Mates and a bit further down by Karmine — the three teams comprise the model’s top three teams in the world. NIP, thanks to their upsets of Vitality and Karmine in Split 2, have gotten close to that top three, while MCI have solidified themselves in fifth place.

Geekay, Kaydop Corp, and GriddyGoose round out the top eight as threatening squads before EU’s depth rosters (including Magnifico and NOVO Esports after a player swap of Bruno Alexandre "AcroniK." dos Santos Lopes and Nico "Nico" Majgren) and bubble teams fill out the remainder of the main event slots.

Only Team Vitality can mathematically clinch on Friday, but one series win more or less locks up a spot (though they are fairly secure already) for the top four, who can each clinch with a Top 4 in Open 6.

MCI will need a bit more to clinch mathematically — a Top 2 will do it — but realistically a Top 6 should be more than enough. More likely than not, they will end the event with the knowledge that they could have not touched their controllers all weekend and still have secured a spot.

Kaydop Corp will clinch a spot outright with an event win — a daunting prospect in the world’s best region — though a somewhat more realistic second place gives them about coinflip odds (relying on MCI to either miss Top 6 or place exactly Top 6 and then lose to KDC in a tiebreaker).

GriddyGoose and Geekay round out the teams with a semirealistic chance to make things interesting, though in Geekay’s case, their current point total (3) makes it difficult for even an event win to be enough unless the bracket goes their way. GriddyGoose (6) can at least put up a mathematical fight if they can survive until the final day of the season.

Geekay’s exceptionally poor results this split have also dragged them into a vulnerable spot in the race for the World Championship. They trail MCI by seven points, a gap that will only expand if and when MCI secures a spot in Paris. Geekay’s only hope of direct qualification and avoiding the LCQ is to win Open 6 outright and hope that MCI flame out in both the open and the Paris Major. The odds are far from being on their side in that regard.

South America

South America’s (to be more specific, FURIA Esports’) struggles on LAN in the summer months of 2025 gave way to another round of roster shuffling prior to RLCS 2026, leaving plenty of questions about how the region’s top dogs would rearrange themselves in what is somewhat of a prove-it year for SAM as a whole.

The biggest move of the offseason was, once again, FURIA shoring up domestic talent by swapping Arthur "DRUFINHO" Langsch Miguel with Team Secret star Pedro Henrique "swiftt." Lewy Gomes.

Around them, a frisky MIBR squad fresh off of a Top 18 at the RLCS 2025 World Championship rebuilt around Victor "Reysbull" Duran Parra by bringing in Luiz Fellipe "Aztromick" Lopes Gomes and Roberto Lima "Sad" de Souza. Other pieces fell into place around the top three, with Celso "wisty" Souza recruiting Maurício "suco" Dalavia and Cauã "crn" Cerione to an Amethyst roster that would finish 3rd-4th in every single event in RLCS 2026 thus far.

Despite a few hiccups (mostly to Team Secret), FURIA once again prevailed as the region’s top dog in Split 1, though it was MIBR, off the back of an Open 2 victory and a 5-0 split record against Team Secret, that took SAM’s second seed heading into Boston.

Though both teams showed fight in Boston (FURIA defeated Team Falcons in the group stage, while MIBR took both Shopify Rebellion and Gentle Mates to Game 5), neither would qualify to the playoffs.

Split 1 was followed by another roster shuffle — most notably, a swap between Secret and Amethyst, who moved to BS+COMPETITION: Secret shipped off four-year veteran Danilo "kv1" Michelini for suco.

Split 2 has only reinforced the two-team dominance over the SAM region, as MIBR and FURIA have twice romped through the bracket to face each other in the grand finals, with each team taking a series. BS+ collected another pair of Top 4 finishes, while Team Secret ended the region’s streak of them, BS+, FURIA, and MIBR making up every Top 4 with an upset loss to Bigodes in the Open 5 quarterfinals.

MIBR’s increasing level of parity with FURIA has quietly moved them to a dead tie on 70 points on the season — a win in Open 6 would, barring an exceptional LAN performance by FURIA, secure the SAM1 seed for the World Championship for the mixed-nationality roster.

Facing near-certain elimination from the Paris Major and World Championship race, Team Secret made a shock announcement ahead of Open 6 that they would be dropping their roster, who moved to the new moniker of BANANA.

Given FURIA and MIBR’s trading of wins in Opens 4 and 5, similar to EU, SAM Open 6 will likely just be a formality in the race for Paris. Both teams sit on 30 points, while BS+ has a mere 16 and BANANA just 14.

RAE Rankings — SAM Open 6

FURIA and MIBR are miles ahead of the rest of South America in RAE’s power rankings — FURIA holds a slight edge, though MIBR has been creeping up on them since the start of the season.

BANANA remains power-ranked SAM3 by a narrow margin despite their loss to SAM5 Bigodes in Open 5 (meaning that had Team Secret held onto the roster, they would have been the present favorites in a potential LCQ). BS+ sits just behind BANANA in rating.

An intriguing Top 8 bubble of NuTorious, Novadrift, 0 esforço fc 2, Dream Esports, Enosis eSports, Emerald, and Overlooked — all of whom have made it to the playoffs in at least one event in RLCS 2026 — will create most of the interest outside of the battle for SAM’s top seed.

Asia-Pacific

Virtus.pro dominated RLCS 2025 in the APAC region, taking all six regionals and putting the region on the map with wins over Wildcard, Shopify Rebellion, and MIBR on LAN.

With the instatement of the two-thirds rule, the majority-import roster was broken up prior to RLCS 2026, with Gianluca "sosa" Petrozza heading back to North America. Kevin "Kevin" Ameye, who played on Elevate during RLCS 2024 and hovered around the APAC top four in RLCS 2025, replaced sosa to fill out the roster alongside Leonardo Raffael "Catalysm" Christ Ramos and Nik Aiman Hakim "Sphinx" Bin Nik Azhar.

With KINOTROPE gaming importing Kurtis "Kash" Cannon to put alongside Caden "furlashh" Shimazawa and perennial contender Max "Maxeew" Ng, the main question headed into Split 1 was whether ex-VP (playing under Project Delacruz) could remain the top dogs in the region.

The consolidation of rising talents Ian "nai" Nomi, risk, and Mohsen Wassiem "valid" Al-Hassan on Ground Zero Gaming (later playing on FOUR GUYS and currently 3CATS) only added to the pressure on PDLC.

Three regional wins answered any questions about contending resoundingly, though KINOTROPE at least consistently played PDLC to lengthy series results, giving at least some hope to other contenders after the LAN-qualifying roster was acquired by TSM and exited Top 16 in Boston.

In Split 2, APAC finally saw a change of pace.

TSM lost in the semifinals to NORTHSTAR, consisting of Josh "Josh" Vannoy (recently changing his name from Lunar), Taha "Taha" Bhatti, and Pengoli. The upset sent reverberations throughout the region, with the main benefactors being KINOTROPE, who secured a regional win and 18 critical split points in Game 7 overtime in the grand finals.

TSM returned to the top in Open 5, defeating KINOTROPE twice and NORTHSTAR once in the playoffs to secure another regional victory, marking their fourth on the season.

While TSM are still fairly secure in the race for APAC’s sole spot at the World Championship, they sit on 26 points (four behind KINOTROPE) for Paris. NORTHSTAR on 20 sit as the only other team alive in the major race. The question remains if the region’s top team — which has been bleeding in its results and rating since the start of the season — can recapture some momentum and prevent KINOTROPE from stealing a LAN spot. TSM still controls its own destiny in doing so.

RAE Rankings — APAC Open 6

TSM are rated as the clear best team in APAC, with KINOTROPE in a clear second place, but the former has been slowly decaying in rating as the season has bore on after Virtus.pro peaked around 1630 at the end of 2025.

3CATS, NORTHSTAR, and a remarkably consistent Jungle Juicers make up the rest of RAE’s top five in APAC, though NORTHSTAR’s inconsistency prevents them from an otherwise clear third place — the roster lost to God Speed in Open 5.

God Speed and Nagatomo Ninjas complete APAC’s B-tier of teams, with the rest of the main event seeing a mix of old talent, one-event wonders, and bubble floaters.

TSM and KINOTROPE are win-and-in, with low odds for both teams if they get eliminated at any point in the tournament. NORTHSTAR have a fighting chance for the Major with an event win, but even then they will have to rely on a poor performance by TSM to do the rest of the heavy lifting.

To summarize, here's who RAE thinks qualifies for the Major out of each region:

  • EU: Team Vitality, Gentle Mates, Karmine Corp, Ninjas in Pyjamas, Manchester City Esports
  • SAM: FURIA Esports, MIBR
  • APAC: TSM

In a strange twist of fate, it is the traditionally top-heavy APAC region, rather than SAM or EU, that has the most intense major race to offer as 23 teams battle for the remaining eight spots in Paris. Most of the scrapping between teams will be for seeding, pride, and points for the World Championship, barring a miracle for someone in SAM or EU. When it comes to miracles, though, the RLCS is no stranger. Geekay Esports pulled off such a feat in 2025; in 2026, they will once again be playing for an improbable victory with their lives on the line. It remains to be seen if the story of the RLCS 2026 online season will have one more twist yet.
