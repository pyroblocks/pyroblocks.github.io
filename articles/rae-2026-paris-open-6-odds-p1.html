RAE Paris Open 6 Preview: NA, MENA, OCE, SSA

Who will advance to the most important major in RLCS history?

April 15, 2026

This article was written in affiliation with ShiftRLE. It may differ somewhat from the style and content of other articles.

The RLCS RAE (Region-Adjusted Elo) Rating system is a predictive model for professional Rocket League, using updating team ratings to generate probabilities for matches, teams, and tournaments. You can read more about it here.

As RLCS 2026 nears its debut on the biggest major stage in the esport's history, four regions are set to send eight teams to the Paris Major in the sixth and final open qualifier of the season.

In a split that's seen traditional regional hierarchies upended around the world, sixteen teams each from North America, Middle East and North Africa, Oceania, and Sub-Saharan Africa will line up to decide who qualifies to the major, with major implications for the race for spots in the RLCS 2026 World Championship.

North America

Split 1 of RLCS 2026 in North America was dominated by two key storylines: the rapid rise of Virtus.pro (then under [REDACTED]) and the falling off of Gen.G Mobil1 Racing. Other than these narratives and the comeups of young Dignitas and M80 rosters, though, it mostly followed the script, with NRG Esports claiming two regional crowns after faltering in the November opener to the season (Shopify Rebellion would win Open 1 at the Kickoff LAN).

The second split in NA has been defined by an early narrative switch in Open 4 that was reinforced tenfold in Open 5, creating one of the most wide-open races for the top spot in North America since the roster of Massimo "Atomic" Franceschi, Landon "BeastMode" Konerman, and Daniel "Daniel" Piecenski joined forces on G2 Stride in 2024.

Open 4 can be succinctly described as Zachary "zach" Stone's (formerly Scrzbbles) homecoming tour. Coming out of retirement after a brief exit from the scene following a Top 8 finish at the RLCS 2025 World Championship, zach's Spacestation Gaming took down M80, the new-look FUT Esports, and NRG on its path to a statement regional win.

SSG made room for zach by dropping Nick "Chronic" Iwanski, who joined Gen.G-expat Justin "jstn." Morales on FUT alongside Carlos "CHEESE." Aguado. FUT, who rostered CHEESE. with Kenneth "frosty" Walz (who went to Gen.G in a trade for jstn.) and Gianluca "sosa" Petrozza (who announced on X that he would take Split 2 off), served as the gatekeepers of NA's Top 4 in the first half of the season — they lost zero series in the Top 16 to teams who failed to qualify for the Boston Major while winning zero against the teams that made it to LAN.

FUT romped their way to a semifinal match against SSG with 10 straight game wins against Freedom, Dignitas, and Strictly Business before the eventual regional champions blocked their upswing in a 2-4 defeat.

The other major narrative in Open 5 was the early exit of Virtus.pro, who stole the show with stellar performances by Cohen "Tawk" Hawco throughout much of Split 1 before going out 9th-12th in Boston. VP would drop their first match of the GSL stage against Dignitas before eventually getting eliminated at the hands of Shopify Rebellion, starting them off on poor footing heading into the rest of the split.

Open 5's GSL stage (with a notably absent M80, who were eliminated 1-3 in the qualifying Swiss Stage) brought a return to normalcy for every team except for one: NRG. The defending world champions took a shock Game 5 overtime defeat to VP in the upper bracket before requiring a reverse sweep over an upstart Next2Nu Esports roster just to qualify for the playoffs.

NRG's struggles would continue on Saturday, requiring six games to put away Strictly Business. On the same day, Gen.G would overcome Dignitas in an ugly seven-game series to advance to their first Top 6 since Open 1, while SSG came back from a 3-1 series deficit to advance past a VP hungry for a bounceback performance. FUT would ultimately steal the show, as their Game 6 win over Rebellion marked the team's first win over an NA Top 4 roster since the start of the season.

Sunday continued to provide more surprises after a predictable set of quarterfinals (in which NRG and Shopify would take down VP and Gen.G, respectively). FUT stunned NA with a five-game rout of NRG in which the longtime trio would score just two goals. The win was especially monumental, as it put the major race into even more distant territory for VP and others as the organization moved to 20 points on the split.

Shopify Rebellion ended SSG's undefeated streak in Top 16s with a 4-2 win in the opposing semifinal, and they would ultimately cap off their Open 5 with a dominant 4-0 sweep over FUT to claim their second regional title of RLCS 2026.

With both Open 4 and Open 5 having the same Top 4 (NRG, SSG, FUT, and SR) and each team having one grand finals appearance and one quarterfinal exit, the gap between the four and the chase pack is almost uncrossable. SR and SSG sit on 26 points and are near-locks to clinch a spot; NRG and FUT comfortably hold 20. The next-highest teams, Strictly Business (10), Virtus.pro (8), and Gen.G (8), are hoping for a regional win and some luck to push them to the Paris Major.

RAE Rankings — NA Open 6

Thanks to struggling (for their standards) in Open 4 and Open 5, NRG has been dropped to near-level with regional winners Rebellion and Spacestation, with FUT not too far behind. VP sits in a close 5th place.

Gen.G, Next2Nu, Dignitas, and SB define the clear third tier in the region, with M80 once again absent from the regional after another Swiss Stage exit. It's been tough going for the top eight bubble this split — across the Open 4 and Open 5 Top 16 stages, only Dignitas has found a win over a top five team (3-1 over VP in Open 4), though Next2Nu has gotten to Champions Field with both Rebellion and NRG this split.

For Rebellion and SSG, the major race is all but won. NRG and FUT sit comfortably if they each make another Top 4 (making their worst-case scenario a tiebreaker with Strictly Business) or Top 6 (where the worst-case would be a tiebreaker with VP or Gen.G, or potentially missing out altogether to SB). Top 8 puts each of them at risk of direct elimination at the hands of VP or Gen.G.

Even with a 1st place finish, Virtus.pro and Gen.G still have worse-than-coinflip odds to qualify to Paris, relying on an aforementioned FUT/NRG Top 6 to force a tiebreaker or Top 8 to qualify outright. The math is a little friendlier for Strictly Business, who are likely to qualify with an event win and have a sliver of a chance with a 2nd place (requiring FUT or NRG to place 9th-12th or worse and for no team with 5-8 points to win the event).

Middle East and North Africa

While ROC Esports turned some heads with their win over Gen.G Mobil1 Racing at the RLCS 2025 World Championship, few expected MENA's status quo to change very much in 2026. That consensus held through the first split of the season, with regional powerhouses Team Falcons and Twisted Minds dropping a combined one domestic series to teams other than themselves through the first three opens.

The one team to take a victory was R8 Esports. R8 are composed of two-thirds of that ROC Esports roster, retaining Abdullah “Abdullah” Alsanie and Ghazi “Ghaazi” Al-Otaibi, replacing Mohammed Zaid “Twiz” Al-Saeed with Next Up graduate Mohammad “M7md” Almutairi, and picking up retired pro Fahad “Zez0nix” Ahmed as coach. R8 took down Twisted Minds in the semifinals of Open 3, opening the door to the MENA Top 2 just a crack.

In Open 4, they blew the hinges off.

After Twisted Minds lost a series in the Swiss Stage to Team Stallions (consisting of Twiz, Muath “Nush” Alotaibi, Turki “Trook” Al-Harbi, and coach Ahmed “Senzo” Ayed), they were forced into a GSL Stage matchup with Team Falcons, which they won 3-0.

R8 followed up with a victory over Twisted Minds in the upper quarterfinals of the playoffs before them and Stallions pulled off back-to-back major upsets over Team Falcons and Twisted, respectively, to make the first MENA grand final without either Mohammed Khalid “trk511” Alotaibi or the twin duo Saleh Abdullah “Rw9” Bakhashwin and Yazeed Abdullah “Kiileerrz” Bakhashwin. R8 would go on to win the event, putting them on strong footing early on in the second split.

Open 5 would see a return to normalcy — that is, for every team besides Team Falcons, who lost to both Twisted and R8 on the road to a second straight 3rd-4th place finish. Twisted would end up conquering R8 in the grand finals, putting the two teams well on their way to the Paris Major.

The race for the major in MENA is down, largely, to four teams: R8 (on 30 split points), Twisted (26), Stallions (18), and Falcons (16).

Falcons' lackluster form has moved them to the second-place team in RAE's MENA rankings, while R8's meteoric rise in the region has them within striking distance of the region's Top 2.

R8 can consider themselves safe with an upper bracket qualification on Thursday (which guarantees at least a tiebreaker with Stallions for a Major spot), and will clinch with a Top 4.

Twisted Minds control their own destiny, and can lock up their spot in Paris with a Top 2. A 3rd-4th puts them at risk of having to play a tiebreaker with Falcons — anything worse drops their odds to below 50%.

Team Falcons have a fighting chance at the major, but their team quality is doing a lot of the heavy lifting — even an event win only gives them a 26% chance to qualify. Their most plausible scenario is a win in the grand finals and a Twisted Minds 3rd-4th; that would lead to a tiebreaker between the two teams.

Stallions have an easier path with an event win (clinching outright if Twisted do not make the grand finals), but the roster's lower quality means that their chances at the major barely exceed 1%.

Lastly, DOS has about a 0.1% chance to qualify out of MENA; they likely need an event win. Currently on 12 points, they need an event win and for Twisted to be eliminated before Saturday — even then, that might only be good enough for a tiebreaker.

Though the most unpredictable split in MENA history has one more chapter yet to unfold, the battle for its last major spot will likely be between its two titans in Twisted Minds and Team Falcons. R8 Esports, who went a combined 1-4 against the two in the first split of RLCS 2026, are the ones who sit in a comfortable spot for qualification.

Oceania

Though OCE has been defying fans' expectations since Wildcard's Top 6 run at the RLCS 2025 World Championship, Split 2 has brought yet more chaos to a region that's been well-known for it.

After PWR took Open 1 over the defending OCE #1 (who then bounced back to claim Open 2), they clinched a spot in Boston — rather anticlimactically — after watching Canterbury-Bankstown Bulldogs sweep Wildcard out of Open 3's semifinals. The Bulldogs, consisting of Caleb “Caleb” Durrant, Josh “kaka” Watters, and Hunter “hntr” Tomeski, proceeded to defeat PWR in the grand finals, giving Oceania its first split with three different event winners since the 2022-23 Spring Split.

PWR took over the mantle as OCE's (mostly) undisputed best team with emphatic back-to-back wins over the Bulldogs (4-1) and Wildcard (4-0) in Open 4. However, after the Bulldogs took down both PWR (in the upper bracket) and Wildcard (in the grand finals, after they defeated PWR in Game 7) in Open 5, the race for OCE's only spot in Paris — and at the World Championship — has been left wide open. With both PWR and the Bulldogs trading off strong weeks, as well as Wildcard still struggling to recapture their 2025 form, it truly will be anyone's game in Open 6.

RAE Rankings — OCE Open 6

Wildcard are still, narrowly, considered to be OCE's best team according to RAE, but both PWR and the Bulldogs are right behind them. The solid rosters of Take Flyte, nerve, and “you not a champ. you a clown.” fill out what has been a fairly strong top six in the region.

The rest of OCE's depth can largely fall to who performs well on the day — the region shuffles rosters more than any other, and consistency can often take a back seat below the region's upper echelon.

Sub-Saharan Africa

Entering RLCS 2026, the perceived hierarchy in SSA post-two-thirds rule was as follows: Pioneers (two-thirds of 2025's Limitless, sans Gareth “Snowyy” Spiers and plus Luis “LuiisP” Puente Peña) on top, Five Fears (consisting of Snowyy, Óscar “tehqoz” Arco, and Next Up graduate Brayden “gunz” Keith) just below, and everyone else playing catch-up.

Though the two mixed-nationality rosters have indeed dominated, combining for 10 grand finals appearances this season, Five Fears has cleanly usurped Pioneers for the top spot in SSA — they claimed Opens 2-5 without dropping a series domestically since their grand finals loss in Open 1.

SSA has had its fair share of chaos in Split 2, but only Five Fears and Pioneers remain alive in the race for both Paris and the 2026 World Championship. With Five Fears claiming both Open 4 and Open 5, though, they've opened up a 12-point lead ahead of the final regional of the season. Pioneers' season is on life support.

RAE Rankings — SSA Open 6

Five Fears and Pioneers are the clear #1 and #2 in SSA, and with formerly third-place Brocrocros dropping Marc “Stake” Bosch Plujà for young gun Kylian “Teschow” Dally and moving to BTF Esports, the gap between the two and the rest of the region is larger than its ever been thus far in RLCS 2026.

After a major shuffle between Open 5 and Open 6, le passion, BTF Esports, ROUNDS, and Death Cloud Esports, followed by Synthesis, Astronic Esports, Looking for Organization, and 7x7, make up the race for SSA's next-best. All besides ROUNDS and Synthesis made a roster change between the penultimate and final open of the season.
